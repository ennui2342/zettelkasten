Distributed practice is the instructional strategy of spreading practice sessions across a course or curriculum rather than teaching a topic exhaustively before moving on. It is the applied complement to the spacing effect: instead of three consecutive lessons on fractions followed by no further work on fractions, fractions are revisited repeatedly across subsequent units.

The educational evidence is strong. Rohrer and colleagues showed that mathematics students who practiced mixed sets of problems — combining review of earlier material with current content — outperformed students who completed blocked problem sets on delayed tests, even when the blocked students performed better during the practice phase itself. The interleaving and return-to-earlier-material structure forces retrieval, not just recognition.

Distributed practice runs against typical curriculum design, which groups related content for convenience. It requires instructional design that deliberately re-introduces earlier material rather than treating each topic as a one-time event. Spaced retrieval exercises embedded in later units — briefly returning to earlier concepts in new contexts — is the practical implementation.

The effect size for distributed over massed practice is substantial and consistent across age groups, content domains, and time scales. It is particularly pronounced for long retention intervals (months rather than days), which corresponds to the actual goal of most education.
