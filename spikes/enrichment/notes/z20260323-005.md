# Transformer Expressivity and Formal Models of Computation

The unique hard attention Transformer (UHAT) serves as a formal abstraction for analyzing the computational power of decoder-only Transformers. In this model, each attention head concentrates its attention on the single position that maximizes the attention score, with ties broken by selecting the rightmost element. This abstraction is motivated by empirical findings that pretrained Transformers tend to concentrate attention on only a few positions. Crucially, UHAT subsumes the expressivity of ordinary softmax Transformers operating at fixed precision, making it a plausible formal model for reasoning about Transformer capabilities in regimes involving long contexts and large reasoning problems.

Each Transformer layer consists of an attention block followed by an MLP block. Multi-head attention with multiple heads concatenates the outputs of individual attention computations and projects them to the model's hidden dimension. The model has access to arbitrary positional embedding vectors for each position within its context window. When analyzing expressivity, a protocol is considered expressible in UHAT if, for each input length, there exists a UHAT Transformer implementing it such that the number of heads and layers remains uniformly bounded across all input lengths, though the width (hidden dimension) may grow. This is a deliberately weak uniformity condition—weaker than requiring a single fixed Transformer across all input lengths, as is common in theoretical work on chain-of-thought reasoning—yet it suffices to establish essentially tight upper and lower bounds on computational costs.

The study of Transformer expressivity connects to a broader literature examining what functions Transformers can and cannot compute, including work on chain-of-thought prompting as a mechanism for extending the computational depth available to a fixed-depth model. Chain-of-thought allows a Transformer to perform sequential computation by generating intermediate tokens autoregressively, effectively turning a constant-depth circuit into one whose depth scales with the number of generated tokens.
