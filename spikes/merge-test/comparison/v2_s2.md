## The Two Lives of Dropout: Regularizer and Bayesian Approximation

Dropout occupies a rare position in deep learning: a single mechanism that admits two distinct theoretical interpretations, each licensing a different inference procedure. The regularization interpretation and the Bayesian interpretation share identical training dynamics — the same mask sampling, the same gradient updates, the same loss landscape. They diverge only at inference time, and this divergence reveals that the Bayesian framing strictly contains the regularization framing.

Under the regularization view, dropout is a noise injection scheme that discourages co-adaptation among neurons. Training builds a single model that implicitly averages over an exponential family of sub-networks. At inference, the noise is removed: all weights are active, scaled to preserve expected activations. The output is a point estimate — the mean of the implicit ensemble. This framing is silent about what the distribution over sub-networks *means* beyond being a training trick.

Under the Bayesian view, the distribution over dropout masks *is* a variational approximation to the posterior over weights. Training with dropout minimizes a specific variational objective (the ELBO, up to a scaling factor related to the dropout rate and weight decay). The sub-network ensemble is not merely a regularization artifact — it is an approximate posterior. This reinterpretation does not change a single line of training code, but it transforms what the trained object *is*: from a regularized point model to an approximate posterior distribution.

The critical divergence is at test time. The regularization framing says: collapse the distribution, use the mean. The Bayesian framing says: *sample from it*. Running multiple stochastic forward passes with dropout active (MC dropout) yields samples from the approximate posterior predictive distribution. The mean of these samples approximates the predictive mean; their variance approximates epistemic uncertainty — the model's uncertainty about its own parameters given finite data. The scaled-weight point prediction is just the expectation of this distribution, discarding all higher moments.

This means the standard inference procedure (dropout off, weights scaled) is a special case of the Bayesian procedure — it recovers the first moment while throwing away uncertainty information. Any practitioner already training with dropout already possesses an approximate Bayesian model; the only question is whether to extract uncertainty from it.

The connection also clarifies the role of dropout hyperparameters. The dropout rate p and the weight decay coefficient jointly determine the prior over weights in the variational interpretation. Tuning these "regularization hyperparameters" is, in Bayesian terms, specifying the prior — a reframing that opens them to principled selection rather than pure cross-validation.

The duality is not perfect. The variational family implied by dropout (Bernoulli multiplicative noise) is restrictive. The approximate posterior may be poorly calibrated for complex posteriors. MC dropout uncertainty estimates can be overconfident compared to full Bayesian methods. But the insight stands: a mechanism designed to prevent overfitting turned out to be performing approximate inference, and recognizing this adds a capability — uncertainty quantification — that the original framing could not provide.
