# Reconstructive Memory: A Concept Carrying Two Contradictory Prescriptions

The standard account of reconstructive memory in cognitive science presents the reconstructive process as a unified phenomenon: recall involves rebuilding a memory trace from fragments rather than reading it out intact, and this process is influenced by current context, schemas, and associated knowledge. This account is correct as a description of the underlying process. But treating it as a single concept conceals a fundamental tension in what the research actually recommends.

In learning and memory research, reconstruction is the engine of consolidation. The practical implication is to induce it deliberately and repeatedly: space practice to force forgetting and reconstruction, require retrieval rather than recognition, reduce feedback so the learner must generate answers. More reconstruction means a more durable trace.

In eyewitness reliability research, reconstruction is the mechanism of contamination. The practical implication is to prevent uncontrolled reconstruction: take statements before post-event information accumulates, use open-ended questions that do not supply suggestive fragments, avoid repeated recall in contaminating contexts. More reconstruction in a suggestive environment means a more distorted trace.

The two prescriptions are not in tension with one another — they are contradictions. Any note that presents both under a unified heading of "reconstructive memory" without sharply separating them will mislead any reader who tries to apply the research. The concept needs to be split: one treatment for reconstruction as consolidation mechanism, one for reconstruction as distortion mechanism, each with its own set of practical implications and links to the relevant literature.
