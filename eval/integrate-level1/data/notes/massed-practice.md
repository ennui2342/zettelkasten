Massed practice — commonly called cramming — concentrates all study of a given topic into a single unbroken session, typically immediately before a test or deadline. It produces strong short-term recall because the material remains continuously active in working memory throughout the session, keeping it above retrieval threshold. However, it fails to produce durable long-term retention.

The failure mechanism is well-understood. During a massed session, each repetition finds the material still accessible from the previous pass. Genuine retrieval — reconstructing a weakened trace from long-term memory — is rarely required. The retrieval practice and spacing mechanisms that drive consolidation are largely bypassed. The trace is refreshed but not strengthened in the way that effortful retrieval would strengthen it.

Empirically, students who massed all study into one session remembered 50–60% less on a delayed test (one week) than students who distributed the same total study time across spaced sessions. The advantage of distributed over massed practice is among the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology.

Massed practice is also resistant to improvement through repetition within the session. Each additional pass over already-active material generates diminishing gains because there is no decay to overcome — no genuine forgetting has occurred. Spacing creates the necessary forgetting that makes each subsequent retrieval effortful and therefore consolidating.

The persistence of cramming as a study strategy reflects metacognitive failure: massed study feels productive because the material becomes fluent within the session, generating a strong subjective sense of mastery. This feeling of fluency is real but misleading — it measures current accessibility, not durable encoding. Students who study via massed practice consistently over-predict their performance on delayed tests.
