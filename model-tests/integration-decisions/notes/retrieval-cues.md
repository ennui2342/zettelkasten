Retrieval from memory is a cue-driven process: recall succeeds when an effective cue is available that can reinstate the mental state present at encoding. The cue does not retrieve the memory directly — it triggers a reconstruction process that uses partial information to rebuild the episode or fact.

Cues can be external (a smell, a location, a word) or internal (a mood, a bodily state, an ongoing mental context). The effectiveness of a cue depends on its informational overlap with the encoded trace: a cue that uniquely specifies the target produces more reliable retrieval than a cue shared by many memory candidates (cue overload). Recognition tasks provide the target itself as its own cue, which is why recognition is generally easier than free recall.

The cue-dependence of retrieval explains why some material feels "on the tip of the tongue" — partially activated but not fully reconstructed. It also explains why retrieval failures in everyday life are often cue failures rather than storage failures: the information is present but cannot be reached with the cues currently available. Changing the context or approaching from a different angle often succeeds.

Elaborative encoding — forming associations between new information and existing knowledge — creates multiple retrieval pathways. Material encoded with many associated cues is accessible via multiple routes and less vulnerable to cue failure.
