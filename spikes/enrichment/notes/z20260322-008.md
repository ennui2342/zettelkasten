# Retrieval-Augmented Generation and Retrieval Corpus Governance

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) grounds large language model outputs in external knowledge corpora, mitigating hallucination and enabling interaction with large technical document collections. In scientific and domain-specific settings, RAG requires careful governance beyond simple document retrieval, encompassing provenance tracking, scoped access, lifecycle management, and quality control of retrieved evidence.

RAG addresses a fundamental limitation of LLMs: their parametric knowledge is fixed at training time, making them prone to factual hallucinations and knowledge gaps. By retrieving relevant information from external sources and integrating it into the generation process, RAG enables models to reason over current, domain-specific, and private data without retraining.

### Retrieval Corpus Design and Governance

A critical architectural choice in RAG systems is the distinction between **global and agent-specific retrieval corpora**. Global corpora contain broadly applicable knowledge—shared documentation, standards, foundational references—while agent-specific corpora are attached to individual agents reflecting narrowly scoped domain expertise: a particular codebase, experimental dataset, or set of technical manuals. This partitioning mirrors how scientific teams intentionally scope expertise, preventing cross-domain contamination and uncontrolled context growth. Enforcement occurs at the execution level: helper agents may only query corpora explicitly assigned to them, and retrieved context is returned to coordinating agents in structured form rather than being shared implicitly across the system.

**Retrieval lifecycle management** is treated as a deliberate, auditable process. Indices are never built, refreshed, or expanded implicitly during system startup or agent execution. Corpus construction and refresh are explicit user actions, ensuring data ingestion events are computationally predictable and temporally traceable. This conservative posture avoids unintended dataset mixing, unclear temporal provenance, and unnecessary recomputation—common error sources in long-lived scientific assistants. Content eligibility for indexing is governed by explicit policy: certain artifact classes (large tabular data, slide decks, ephemeral analysis outputs) are designated as on-demand resources and excluded from indexing even if they reside within retrieval paths, preventing semantic dilution and uncontrolled index growth.

Post-retrieval processing includes **aggregation and filtering policies** such as per-source or per-document caps, diversity constraints, and agent-scoped supersampling rules that prioritize evidence from designated knowledge roots. These mechanisms ensure downstream reasoning operates over bounded, interpretable evidence sets rather than unstructured accumulations of retrieved text, supporting both long-horizon reasoning stability and traceable evidence use.

### Open Challenges in Retrieval Governance

Remaining challenges include incremental and verifiable indexing pipelines (content-hash-based updates, dataset version pinning, index-level provenance), handling corpus drift in frequently changing knowledge bases, and provenance-aware summarization for long-term memory compaction. Hybrid retrieval strategies combining dense and lexical methods are commonly employed, with document-centric retrieval pipelines demonstrating how structured evidence assembly improves reasoning quality in technical domains. Orchestration frameworks supporting agent coordination and asynchronous state management help manage complexity across retrieval stages.
