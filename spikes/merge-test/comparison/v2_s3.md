## Cosine Similarity and the Normalised Dot Product in Embedding Retrieval

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors in a high-dimensional space, independent of their magnitudes. It is defined as the dot product of two vectors divided by the product of their L2 norms:

$$\text{sim}(A, B) = \frac{A \cdot B}{\|A\| \, \|B\|}$$

The result ranges from −1 (diametrically opposite directions) through 0 (orthogonality) to 1 (identical direction). The key property is scale invariance: similarity depends only on the proportional distribution of values across dimensions, not on overall magnitude. In text applications, this means two documents are judged similar if they discuss the same topics in the same proportions, regardless of length. The same principle extends to any domain where embeddings encode semantic content.

The term "normalised dot product" refers to exactly the same operation. A raw dot product conflates directional similarity with magnitude; dividing by the norms isolates the angular relationship. The two names — cosine similarity and normalised dot product — are mathematically identical and fully interchangeable. The distinction is purely one of framing: some libraries and papers foreground the geometric interpretation (cosine of the angle), while others emphasise the algebraic form (a dot product that has been normalised).

This equivalence has a direct practical consequence. If all embedding vectors are L2-normalised once at index time, every subsequent cosine similarity computation reduces to a plain dot product, since the denominator becomes 1. Plain dot products are exactly what optimised retrieval libraries — FAISS, HNSW-based indexes — are built to accelerate via SIMD instructions and approximate nearest-neighbour structures (MIPS: maximum inner product search). The result is cosine similarity semantics at maximum hardware speed.

In production, vector databases such as Pinecone, Weaviate, and Qdrant handle this normalisation transparently when the cosine metric is selected. Likewise, some embedding providers (e.g., the OpenAI embeddings API) return pre-normalised vectors, so retrieval can proceed with dot product directly. Other frameworks — scikit-learn, sentence-transformers — expose the metric explicitly as cosine similarity and normalise internally. Regardless of interface, the underlying computation is the same.

Cosine similarity became the dominant metric in TF-IDF retrieval and carried over naturally into dense retrieval, semantic search, and recommendation systems. Its scale invariance, mathematical simplicity, and compatibility with fast inner-product search make it the default choice for nearly all embedding-based comparison tasks.
