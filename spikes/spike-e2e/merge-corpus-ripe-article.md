# Active Recall and Retrieval Practice Are the Same Thing

The self-directed learning community and the cognitive psychology literature have independently arrived at the same core insight and given it different names. "Active recall" is the term used in flashcard communities, productivity writing, and popular learning guides. "Retrieval practice" is the term used in experimental psychology, education research, and the desirable difficulties literature. They describe identical behaviour — attempting to retrieve target information from memory without consulting the source — and they explain that behaviour through identical mechanisms.

The distinction, such as it is, is purely sociological. Active recall is the term adopted by practitioners and self-help writers who developed the technique empirically, often without reference to the academic literature. Retrieval practice is the term used by researchers who study it under controlled conditions. Both communities agree on the core finding: generating information from memory produces stronger retention than re-studying the same information. Both agree on the mechanism: effortful retrieval strengthens the memory trace by forcing reconstruction. Both agree on the practical implication: replace re-reading and passive review with generation and testing.

Maintaining two separate notes for active recall and retrieval practice creates a false impression that these are distinct concepts with separate literatures and separate evidence bases. They do not. The evidence base is shared — every experimental demonstration of the testing effect is simultaneously evidence for active recall, and every practical implementation of active recall is an instance of retrieval practice. Any knowledge base that represents them as separate entries will mislead readers about the structure of this area of cognitive science.

The appropriate treatment is a single note that acknowledges both terms, explains their common origin in different communities, and presents the shared mechanism and shared evidence under a unified heading. Practitioners using "active recall" and researchers using "retrieval practice" are working with the same tool. Their notes should be one note.
