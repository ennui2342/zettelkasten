# Phase Transitions in Hierarchical Aggregation as the Information-Theoretic Foundation for Multi-Agent Communication Regimes

The three qualitative regimes of multi-agent reasoning—communication-free, beneficial, and communication-intensive partitioning—are not ad hoc empirical categories but manifestations of a single phase-transition phenomenon in hierarchical information aggregation. The bridging insight: **the sharp amplification–collapse dichotomy governed by the critical scalar λ is the microscopic dynamics whose macroscopic manifestation is the regime structure of multi-agent reasoning**. A central complication, and the reason naive scaling often fails, is that agents derived from the same base model, training data, or prompt template exhibit positively correlated errors—a phenomenon sometimes called "groupthink"—which fundamentally limits aggregation benefits and can push systems from amplifying regimes into collapsing ones.

### The aggregation model

Model a hierarchical multi-agent system as a DAG where leaf agents observe input partitions and internal nodes aggregate subordinate outputs via majority-like operations. Each inter-agent edge is a lossy channel parameterized by message length $m$: for binary decisions, a binary symmetric channel with reliability $\eta(m)$; for continuous tasks, additive distortion with variance $\sigma_c^2(m)$. Both improve with $m$ but at greater cost.

When subordinates share failure modes—overlapping context, common prompt vulnerabilities, identical training distributions—their outputs exhibit shared-failure correlation $\rho \in [0,1]$. For binary tasks, $\rho$ equals the pairwise correlation of signed correctness variables $Z_i = Y \cdot \hat{Y}_i$ (ground truth times prediction); for continuous tasks, it is the pairwise correlation of residuals. A concrete generative construction—the $\rho$-shared correlation model—produces this correlation exactly: with probability $\rho$, all children in an aggregation group share a single common vote (groupthink mode), and with probability $1-\rho$, they vote independently. This mixture yields a closed-form correlated aggregation map:

$$F_\rho(b) = \rho \cdot \eta b + (1-\rho) \cdot \eta \cdot M_k(b)$$

which linearly interpolates between the identity function (pure groupthink, where aggregation adds nothing) and the independent majority map (maximum amplification). The recursion governs whether a weak ground-truth signal at the leaves survives aggregation or decays to noise, with critical condition:

$$\lambda = \eta\bigl[(1-\rho)\,M_k'(0) + \rho\bigr] > 1.$$

### Correlated failures and effective ensemble size

Correlation directly weakens the amplification capacity of aggregation. As $\rho$ increases toward 1, $\lambda$ approaches $\eta \leq 1$, meaning the system behaves as if it has only one effective voter regardless of fan-in. This can push $\lambda$ below 1, causing deep hierarchies to collapse to chance.

For continuous averaging, the impact is equally stark. Under equal pairwise correlation $\rho$, averaging $n$ agents with individual variance $\sigma^2$ yields MSE $\sigma^2[\rho + (1-\rho)/n]$. As $n \to \infty$, the MSE converges to $\rho \sigma^2$ rather than zero—an irreducible floor representing the variance of the shared error component that no amount of scaling can eliminate. The effective number of independent agents is approximately $1/\rho$ when $\rho$ is moderate, explaining why large ensembles of similar models often behave like a small number of truly independent estimators. This is a fundamental departure from classical ensemble theory, where errors are assumed independent and aggregation benefits grow without bound.

### λ maps onto regime boundaries

**Communication-free tasks: deep supercritical (λ ≫ 1).** Each agent's local observation is independently informative ($\rho \approx 0$) and per-edge fidelity $\eta$ is high relative to fan-in $k$. The bias recursion converges rapidly to a high fixed point $b^*$; minimal communication suffices. The task decomposes cleanly because the amplification condition is easily satisfied.

**Beneficial-partitioning tasks: near but above criticality (λ ≳ 1).** Composing partial results requires each aggregation step to preserve enough bias. The system is supercritical, so information propagates, but mixing depth scales as $O(\log(1/b_0)/\log\lambda)$ and performance is sensitive to reductions in $\eta$ or increases in $\rho$. The benefit of partitioning arises because distributing computation reduces per-agent depth while hierarchical amplification preserves accuracy—but the communication budget must keep $\eta$ above threshold, and homogeneous agents sharing identical prompts push $\rho$ upward toward collapse. This regime is where correlated failures are most dangerous: a modest increase in $\rho$ can silently push $\lambda$ below 1, converting a functioning system into one that degrades with scale.

**Communication-intensive tasks: at or below criticality (λ ≤ 1).** Multi-hop reasoning scatters information across partitions, yielding low initial bias $b_0$ and high effective $\rho$. In the subcritical case, bias decays geometrically to zero with depth regardless of tree structure. Bandwidth determines $\eta$, and when $\eta$ drops below $\eta^* = 1/[(1-\rho)\,M_k'(0) + \rho]$, information propagation ceases. Remedies: increase $\eta$ (longer messages), decrease $\rho$ (diversify agent strategies), or increase $k$ (wider fan-in, though bounded by $M_k'(0) \leq \sqrt{k}$).

### Communication bottlenecks and architectural constraints

**Multi-hop loss compounding.** In chain topologies without new evidence, each hop multiplicatively attenuates signal: binary bias decays as $\eta^L b_0$ over $L$ hops; continuous MSE grows as $\sigma^2 + L\cdot\sigma_c^2(m)$. Pure relay chains are strictly signal-degrading—sequential handoff helps only when intermediate nodes contribute independent computation.

**Irreducible performance floors.** Even with arbitrarily many leaves, communication loss across layers produces floors: $\sigma_c^2(m)/(k-1)$ for continuous tasks, and the fixed point $b^*$ for binary tasks. Correlation imposes an additional, often more binding floor: $\rho \sigma^2$ for continuous averaging, which persists even with perfect communication. These floors are lowered only by increasing message length, fan-in, or—critically—reducing $\rho$, not by adding depth or more homogeneous agents.

**Context windows as fan-in constraints.** A finite context window $W$ limits any aggregation node to $\lfloor W/m \rfloor$ inputs. Flat architectures saturate at a fixed agent count; hierarchical trees bypass this globally by enforcing $km \leq W$ locally, enabling $n = k^d$ leaves through depth $d$ at the cost of multi-hop loss. Context saturation—where concatenating agent traces degrades performance—is this constraint binding: adding agents dilutes usable signal once the aggregator is context-full.

**Message length as design variable.** Length $m$ creates a three-way trade-off: longer messages improve fidelity but reduce feasible fan-in and increase cost. Optimal $m$ is monotonically nondecreasing in total budget (via increasing-differences of the log-objective), enabling efficient design-curve computation through a linear-time upper-envelope algorithm.

### Reducing correlation as a primary design lever

When performance plateaus despite scaling agent count, the theory identifies correlation reduction as the primary lever for improvement rather than adding more agents. The effective per-layer gain $\lambda = \eta[(1-\rho)M_k'(0) + \rho]$ makes clear that reducing $\rho$ has a multiplicative effect on the amplification term $(1-\rho)M_k'(0)$, which dominates the gain when fan-in is large.

Practical mechanisms for reducing $\rho$ include using diverse base models, varying prompts or temperature settings, incorporating different tool policies, or adding explicit verification and critique roles. Structured protocols such as debate or adversarial verification can be interpreted as mechanisms that effectively reduce $\rho$ by forcing agents to explore different reasoning paths, though they typically increase coordination cost. The framework provides a concrete evaluation criterion for such protocols: measure how much they reduce the effective $\rho$ per unit of additional budget, and check whether this moves the system across the supercritical boundary $\lambda > 1$ or the synergy boundary where collaborative gains exceed individual costs.

Empirically, shared failure modes have been identified as a dominant bottleneck in large-scale evaluations of LLM agent systems, where naive aggregation can amplify rather than cancel errors. The theoretical prediction that high $\rho$ makes scaling out counterproductive aligns with observations that multi-agent variants degrade on tasks where the base model has systematic blind spots—precisely the setting where $\rho$ is highest.

### The design trilemma through the lens of λ

The critical scalar encodes three design levers: communication fidelity $\eta$ (token and latency cost), decorrelation $1-\rho$ (requiring heterogeneous agents, increasing design complexity), and fan-in $k$ (parallelism, subject to diminishing returns from $M_k'(0) \leq \sqrt{k}$ and context-window limits). Expanding the feasible region of the performance–cost–efficiency trilemma amounts to engineering $\lambda > 1$ at minimal aggregate cost.

The correlation parameter $\rho$ deserves special emphasis as a design variable because it is often the binding constraint in practice. Adding agents is cheap; making them genuinely independent is hard. The effective ensemble size $n_{\text{eff}} \approx 1/\rho$ imposes a ceiling that no amount of horizontal scaling can breach. This reframes the scaling question: the relevant metric is not how many agents a system deploys but how many effectively independent perspectives it maintains.

Procedural memory contributes on both fronts: by increasing effective $\eta$—agents with accumulated execution knowledge transmit more informative compressed messages—and by reducing $\rho$, since diverse accumulated experiences decorrelate failures among architecturally similar agents. An orchestrator with procedural memory effectively performs learned aggregation, partially closing the gap between majority-vote and Bayes-optimal thresholds.

### Structural guarantees

The concavity of $F_\rho$ ensures that once $\lambda > 1$, the fixed point $b^*$ is unique and globally attracting: asymptotic accuracy is robust to perturbations. This corresponds to the practical observation that supercritical systems show stable performance across instances, while near-critical systems exhibit high variance—a hallmark of critical phenomena. The Kesten–Stigum analogy further clarifies that majority-vote aggregation is computationally simple but information-theoretically suboptimal; the gap between majority-vote and Bayes-optimal thresholds quantifies the cost of simple aggregation, partially recoverable by increasing fan-in, improving fidelity, reducing correlation, or replacing majority vote with learned aggregation.

The $\rho$-shared correlation model's mixture construction provides an additional structural guarantee: because $F_\rho$ is a convex combination of the identity and the independent majority map, all qualitative properties (concavity, fixed-point uniqueness, threshold behavior) transfer cleanly from the independent case, with $\rho$ acting as a continuous interpolation parameter that smoothly degrades the system from full ensemble benefit to no ensemble benefit.
