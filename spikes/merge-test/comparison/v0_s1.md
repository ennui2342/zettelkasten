## Dense Retrieval and Semantic Search Are the Same Technique Under Different Names

Dense retrieval and semantic search refer to the same core method: encode texts into dense vectors using a learned encoder, index those vectors for efficient nearest-neighbor lookup, and rank candidates by geometric proximity (cosine similarity or dot product) to a query vector. The architecture, training objectives, and inference pipeline are identical. The two terms arose because the information retrieval (IR) and natural language processing (NLP) communities developed and named the technique independently, each framing it against a different baseline.

The IR framing — "dense retrieval" — positions the method against sparse retrieval (BM25, TF-IDF), emphasizing the shift from high-dimensional, sparse term-frequency vectors to low-dimensional, learned dense representations. The NLP framing — "semantic search" — positions the method against surface-level or keyword matching, emphasizing that the embedding space captures meaning: paraphrases, synonyms, and conceptual relatives land in nearby regions regardless of lexical overlap.

These are not competing emphases but complementary descriptions of the same property. Sparse retrieval fails on vocabulary mismatch precisely because it operates on surface form; dense retrieval succeeds on vocabulary mismatch precisely because it captures semantics. Saying "dense, not sparse" and saying "semantic, not lexical" are two ways of pointing at the same architectural choice and the same resulting behavior.

The convergence became explicit with Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR), which both communities recognized as foundational, and with the BEIR benchmark, which evaluated systems described under both labels using a single protocol. Yet the terminological split has had real costs: hard negative mining, in-batch negative sampling, and training efficiency techniques were independently developed (and sometimes independently published) in both communities, with incomplete cross-citation. Recognizing the equivalence is not merely a matter of tidiness — it directly reduces duplicated effort and makes the combined literature navigable as a single body of work.

The unifying principle is that a bi-encoder trained on large text corpora learns a vector space where geometric proximity encodes semantic relatedness, and this single fact is what both "dense retrieval" and "semantic search" name. Any distinction between the two is purely one of rhetorical framing, not of mechanism.
