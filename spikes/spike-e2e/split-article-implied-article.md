# State-Dependent Memory: Two Phenomena Sharing a Label

The term "state-dependent memory" covers two quite different phenomena that have been studied in different research traditions, have different practical implications, and arguably warrant separate treatment in any systematic account of memory.

## Drug-Induced State Dependence

The original demonstrations of state-dependent memory used pharmacological manipulations: rats trained while intoxicated performed better when tested intoxicated than when tested sober; humans who learned material under the influence of alcohol or sedatives recalled it better in the same pharmacological state. This is a robust finding, but its practical implications are narrow. The pharmacological version of state-dependent memory tells us that retrieval contexts should match encoding contexts — a point also made by context-dependent memory research — but it does not generate useful instructional prescriptions because the "state" in question (drug intoxication) is not something educators, therapists, or learners should deliberately induce. The finding is theoretically interesting but practically inert for most applications outside addiction research and forensic toxicology.

## Mood-Congruent and Mood-Dependent Memory

The emotional version of state-dependent memory is a different matter. Mood-dependent memory refers to the finding that people remember more when their mood at retrieval matches their mood at encoding — material learned while sad is better recalled when sad, material learned while happy is better recalled when happy. Mood-congruent memory, a related but distinct finding, refers to the tendency to recall emotionally consistent material more readily regardless of encoding conditions — a depressed person finds it easier to retrieve memories of failure, loss, and embarrassment than memories of success.

These mood-based effects have direct and important implications for clinical psychology, therapeutic practice, and the understanding of depression and anxiety. A depressed patient does not simply feel bad — they are caught in a retrieval loop where their current emotional state preferentially activates negative memories, which reinforces the depression, which further biases retrieval. This is a fundamentally different practical concern from the question of whether information learned under alcohol is better retrieved under alcohol.

## The Cost of Conflation

Grouping pharmacological and emotional state-dependence under a single heading of "state-dependent memory" obscures these distinct implications. A clinician reading about state-dependent memory in the context of depression needs the emotional/mood account; the pharmacological findings are at best irrelevant and at worst misleading. A researcher in forensic toxicology needs the pharmacological account; the mood literature is a distraction.

The shared mechanism — that matching internal state at encoding and retrieval improves recall — is real and worth noting. But the two bodies of research have developed separately, apply to different populations, and generate different practical guidance. A note that treats them as a unified topic should at minimum clearly distinguish the two, and a strong case can be made that they should be maintained as separate entries with an explicit link capturing the shared mechanism.
