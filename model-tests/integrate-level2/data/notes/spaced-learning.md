Spaced learning refers to the practice of distributing study or retrieval sessions across time rather than concentrating them. Memory strength decays exponentially after initial encoding (Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve), and each successful retrieval resets the curve with a shallower slope. By timing reviews to arrive just before the memory would be lost, the learner maximises the effect of each review session.

The contrast is with massed practice, where the same total study time is concentrated in a single session. Massed practice produces strong immediate performance because the material stays active throughout, but fails to produce durable encoding because genuine retrieval — pulling a trace back from long-term memory — is never required. Spaced learning makes each retrieval session effortful, which is precisely what drives consolidation.

Cepeda et al. (2006) conducted a meta-analysis across 254 studies covering over 14,000 participants and found that spaced practice consistently outperformed massed practice for long-term retention across all material types, age groups, and retention intervals studied. The optimal spacing gap increases with the retention interval: material to be remembered for a week benefits from gaps of roughly one day; material to be remembered for a year benefits from gaps of weeks.

The spacing effect is one of the most robust and replicated findings in cognitive psychology, and one of the most consistently under-utilised in practice. Learners systematically underestimate its benefit and overestimate the value of massed review sessions because massed review produces stronger immediate fluency.
