## Dense Retrieval and Semantic Search

Dense retrieval and semantic search are two names for the same underlying technique: encoding queries and documents into dense vector representations, indexing those vectors for efficient lookup, and ranking candidates by geometric proximity in the resulting embedding space. The two labels emerged from different research communities — information retrieval (IR) and natural language processing (NLP), respectively — but refer to identical architecture, training objectives, and inference procedures.

The core mechanism is a bi-encoder that maps texts into a shared vector space learned from large corpora. Semantic similarity corresponds to geometric closeness: texts with related meaning cluster together regardless of lexical overlap. A query about "car safety" retrieves documents mentioning "vehicle crash protection" because both occupy nearby regions of the space. This property makes the approach robust to vocabulary mismatch, synonymy, paraphrase, and out-of-vocabulary terms — all cases where traditional sparse methods such as BM25 struggle.

In practice, exact nearest-neighbour search over the full index is prohibitively expensive, so retrieval is approximate. Libraries such as FAISS and graph-based indices such as HNSW provide sub-linear search, trading a small amount of recall for large gains in latency. The technique is widely deployed in open-domain question answering, enterprise search, document retrieval, and recommendation systems.

The terminological split between "dense retrieval" and "semantic search" reflects a difference in emphasis rather than substance. IR researchers frame the method in contrast to sparse retrieval, highlighting the shift from term-matching representations to learned dense representations. NLP researchers emphasise the semantic nature of the similarity captured by the embeddings, distinguishing it from surface-level or syntactic matching. The convergence became explicit with Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR, Karpukhin et al., 2020), recognised in both communities as an embedding-based approach, and was further cemented by the BEIR benchmark (Thakur et al., 2021), which evaluated systems described under both labels within a single unified protocol.

This divergence in vocabulary has had practical consequences. Both communities independently developed closely related techniques — hard negative mining, in-batch negative sampling, strategies for improving training efficiency — sometimes without awareness of parallel work on the other side. Effective dense retrieval requires large-scale pretraining to capture semantic structure, and much of the progress in training methodology has been duplicated across the two lines of research. Adopting a unified vocabulary would reduce redundancy and accelerate progress in the field.
