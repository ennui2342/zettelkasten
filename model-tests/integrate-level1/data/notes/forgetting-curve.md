The forgetting curve, first described by Ebbinghaus (1885) using syllable lists he memorised and re-tested on himself, shows that memory strength decays exponentially following initial encoding. Retention drops steeply in the first hours after learning, then the rate of loss slows: roughly 50% of information is lost within an hour, 70% within a day, and 80% within a week, given no further review.

The shape of the curve is not fixed — it reflects the strength of the original encoding. Material learned under conditions of deep processing, emotional salience, or repeated retrieval has a shallower decay slope from the outset. Weakly encoded material decays more steeply.

Each successful retrieval resets the curve with a shallower slope than before. After the first review, a longer period passes before the same level of forgetting occurs. After each subsequent review, the interval before the next necessary review extends further. This is the empirical basis for spaced repetition scheduling: reviews timed to arrive just before the memory would fall below a retrieval threshold require fewer total review sessions than reviews at fixed short intervals.

Ebbinghaus also documented the savings effect: re-learning previously forgotten material takes fewer trials than the original learning, even when the material cannot be consciously recalled at all. This shows that forgetting is not total erasure — some trace persists below the threshold of explicit retrieval.

The practical implication for study design is that review timing matters at least as much as total review time. A single long session early in learning is outperformed by shorter sessions distributed across the decay curve.
