# Generation Effect

The generation effect is the finding that producing information yourself — generating an answer, completing a sentence, constructing an explanation — produces substantially stronger memory than reading or receiving the same information passively. The effect holds even when generation is easy and constrained, and even when the time spent is equivalent.

The mechanism involves encoding depth. Generating a response requires the learner to activate semantic knowledge, evaluate candidates against meaning constraints, and construct a relationship between the cue and the target. This is semantically richer processing than registering the surface form of a presented answer. The generation act engages the conceptual structure underlying the information rather than its surface presentation.

This explains why writing summaries in your own words is more effective than highlighting or transcribing. Highlighting and transcription require only shallow processing — identifying and copying a target — while summarisation in your own words requires understanding well enough to reconstruct from scratch. The quality of the resulting memory reflects the quality of the processing, not the quantity of exposure.

Generation also interacts with error. When a learner generates an incorrect response that is then corrected, the memory for the correct answer is often stronger than if the correct answer had been simply presented. The effort invested in the wrong direction — and the correction that follows — produces distinctive encoding. This is sometimes called the hypercorrection effect.
