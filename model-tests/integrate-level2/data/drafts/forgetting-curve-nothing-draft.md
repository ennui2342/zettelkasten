# The Forgetting Curve

Ebbinghaus (1885) documented that memory strength decays exponentially over time following encoding. Retention drops steeply in the first hours and days, then the rate of loss slows. Using nonsense syllable lists memorised and self-tested across varying delays, he showed that roughly half of learned material is lost within an hour of study with no further review.

The shape of the decay curve is not fixed — material encoded under deep or emotionally salient conditions has a shallower slope from the outset. Each successful retrieval resets the curve with a shallower slope than before, meaning each review extends the interval before the next review is needed.

Ebbinghaus also found that re-learning previously forgotten material takes fewer trials than the original learning, even when the material cannot be explicitly recalled. This savings effect shows that forgetting does not mean erasure — a residual trace persists below the threshold of conscious retrieval.

The practical implication is that review timing matters as much as total review time. Reviews timed to arrive just before the memory would be lost achieve maximum consolidation per session; reviews that arrive too early, when the trace is still strong, produce diminishing returns.
