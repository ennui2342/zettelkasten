# AI Alignment with Human Preferences

Aligning AI systems—particularly LLM-based agents—with human preferences is a central challenge that intensifies when multiple agents must cooperate, making alignment a system-level objective. A key theme is the shift from opaque reward signals toward structured, interpretable evaluation instruments—most notably rubrics—that decompose preferences into verifiable, human-understandable dimensions.

**Training-time alignment** has been dominated by RLHF and variants like DPO, which removes the explicit reward model by directly optimizing likelihood ratios. These approaches optimize against fixed training-time preferences and are vulnerable to reward over-optimization. Foundational critiques show that Bradley–Terry assumptions (transitivity, independence of irrelevant alternatives) are systematically violated in realistic preference spaces. Policy optimization methods such as Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) and Group Sequence Policy Optimization (GSPO) treat generation as sequential decision-making: GSPO replaces token-level importance ratios with length-normalized sequence-level geometric means for more stable optimization of variable-length outputs, with KL divergence enforcing a trust region around a reference policy. Two-phase training curricula combine supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with reinforcement fine-tuning, where SFT bootstraps structured output generation and RL yields statistically significant gains—most pronounced in subjective, multi-dimensional domains like content creation and legal compliance. Prioritized experience replay adapted to this setting selectively replays low-return episodes, improving sample efficiency by focusing on failure cases.

**Test-time reward modeling** addresses training-time rigidity by evaluating outputs dynamically at inference. Generative Reward Models (GenRM) and foundation models like GRAM produce natural-language evaluations rather than fixed scalars. Multi-objective extensions and Directional Preference Alignment allow controllable trade-offs across reward dimensions. Best-of-N sampling demonstrates consistent improvement as candidate count increases, with each doubling of N yielding approximately constant absolute gains—and learned evaluation functions can exhibit scaling slopes nearly identical to oracle evaluators, suggesting well-trained proxies approximate true utility even without ground-truth access. Despite increased flexibility, these methods remain opaque without revealing which criteria drive evaluation.

**Rubric-based evaluation** makes reward models transparent by decomposing preferences into weighted, verifiable natural-language criteria spanning factual accuracy, reasoning depth, structural compliance, and stylistic quality. Related approaches include mixture-of-experts reward architectures with interpretable components and sparse autoencoders disentangling reward representations into semantic latent factors. A well-designed rubric organizes criteria into complementary stages: gate criteria (structure/compliance, ~20–30% weight), verification criteria (factual/logical correctness, ~40–50%), and quality criteria (clarity, tone, usefulness, ~20–30%), typically 9–12 criteria total. Effective criteria are specific, verifiable, independent, and allow partial credit. The proxy utility is a linearly additive weighted sum of criterion scores.

**Rubric verification** combines deterministic code-based checks (schema validation, arithmetic, format) with model-based judges (factual consistency, argument quality, tone). Best practices include using verifier models from different families than systems being evaluated and designing criteria that exploit verification asymmetry—checking a property is easier than generating outputs satisfying it.

**Rubric generation as a learning problem** treats rubric creation as policy optimization. A rubric decomposer maps task descriptions and preference statements into structured rubrics through interactive elicitation, trained via SFT on synthetic dialogues followed by reinforcement fine-tuning balancing rubric fidelity against interaction costs and compute costs. Reward shaping incorporates auxiliary terms: clarification cost modeling stakeholder cognitive burden via logarithmic normalization, and compute cost capturing API and wall-clock expenses with logarithmic saturation curves. Reinforcement fine-tuning produces rubrics with more concise, imperative phrasing, stable weight distributions, and lower variance in criterion length. Large-scale synthetic frameworks demonstrate rubric generation across diverse domains without extensive human labeling. Practical training uses parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA), mixed-precision training, and quantization on single high-end GPUs with gradient accumulation.

**Functional alignment** provides theoretical grounding: a proxy reward is functionally aligned if it is ordinally equivalent to the stakeholder's true utility—preserving the same preference ordering. Following Debreu's representation theorem, training with a strictly proper loss on the same decoding support used at test time recovers the correct ordering up to monotone transformation, regardless of supervision format (pointwise, pairwise, or listwise). This applies equally to scalar reward models and rubric-based proxies, provided weighted criterion scores preserve the true ordering.

**Configurable test-time alignment** allows stakeholders to inspect, edit, or override criteria and weights at inference without retraining. Rubrics' decomposed structure makes this directly feasible. Strategies including best-of-N sampling, importance reweighting, and tree/beam search guided by rubric scores enable test-time scaling with transparent post-hoc human control.
