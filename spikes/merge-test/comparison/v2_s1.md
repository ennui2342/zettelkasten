## Dense Retrieval and Semantic Search via Neural Embedding Spaces

Dense retrieval and semantic search refer to the same underlying technique: encoding queries and documents into a shared vector space using neural networks, then ranking candidates by geometric proximity under a distance metric such as cosine similarity or dot product. The two terms reflect different disciplinary framings — information retrieval researchers emphasise the contrast with sparse methods like BM25 and call it "dense retrieval" or "bi-encoder retrieval," while natural language processing researchers emphasise the capture of meaning beyond surface form and call it "semantic search" or "embedding-based search." The architecture, training objective, and inference procedure are identical across both framings.

The core property is that semantic similarity maps to geometric closeness in the learned embedding space. Texts that share meaning cluster together regardless of lexical overlap: a query about "car safety" retrieves documents mentioning "vehicle crash protection" because both occupy nearby regions of the space. This capacity to handle vocabulary mismatch is the principal advantage over keyword-based retrieval, which fails on out-of-vocabulary queries, paraphrases, and domain-specific synonyms.

The standard architecture is a bi-encoder: separate encoder networks produce fixed-dimensional vectors for queries and documents, allowing document embeddings to be precomputed and indexed offline. At query time, only the query is encoded, and approximate nearest-neighbour search — typically using HNSW or FAISS indices — retrieves candidates in sub-linear time. This decoupling of encoding and retrieval is what makes the approach practical at scale.

Effective embedding spaces require large-scale pretraining on text corpora, often followed by fine-tuning with contrastive objectives that push relevant pairs together and irrelevant pairs apart. Techniques such as hard negative mining and in-batch negatives improve the discriminative quality of the space. DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020) was a landmark system that demonstrated strong performance in open-domain question answering and was recognised in both the IR and NLP communities as an embedding-based approach. The BEIR benchmark (Thakur et al., 2021) later evaluated dense retrieval and semantic search systems under a single protocol, confirming they are instances of the same method.

Applications span open-domain question answering, enterprise document search, and recommendation systems. Dense retrieval outperforms sparse methods most clearly when queries diverge from document vocabulary, though it depends on sufficient pretraining data to capture semantic structure. The historical terminological split between the IR and NLP communities led to duplicated research effort — both independently developed overlapping techniques for training efficiency, negative sampling, and evaluation — and a unified vocabulary would have accelerated progress.
