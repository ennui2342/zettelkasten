# Sleep and Memory Consolidation

Sleep plays an active role in memory consolidation. During slow-wave sleep, the hippocampus replays patterns of neural activity from waking experience, transferring recent episodic memories to neocortical storage. This process — called hippocampal-neocortical dialogue or offline replay — stabilises newly encoded memories and integrates them with prior knowledge.

REM sleep contributes a different consolidation function, particularly for procedural and emotional memories. Emotional memories are processed during REM, with the emotional valence sometimes dampened while the factual content is preserved — a mechanism thought to underlie the therapeutic value of sleep after traumatic events.

The practical implication is that sleep deprivation after learning significantly impairs consolidation: memories formed just before a period of sleep deprivation show much larger losses than memories followed by normal sleep, even when the total waking review time is equivalent. A 90-minute nap after a learning session can produce nearly as much consolidation benefit as a full night of sleep.
