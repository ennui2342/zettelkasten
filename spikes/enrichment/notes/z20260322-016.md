# Multi-Agent Systems with Large Language Models

Multi-agent systems (MAS) built on LLMs distribute complex tasks across specialized agents with distinct roles, offering a scalable alternative to monolithic model scaling. The MAS pipeline has three stages: **agent selection** (routing tasks to expert subsets), **inter-agent collaboration**, and **result aggregation** (voting, self-assessment, or hierarchical fusion). Most prior work treats collaboration as a black box—a gap newer frameworks explicitly address.

### Collaboration Structures and Debate

Interaction structures range from **pre-defined topologies** (AutoGen, ChatEval) that fix communication paths, to **group discussion** (ReConcile) using confidence-based consensus, to **Multi-Agent Debate (MAD)** with structured argumentation and meta-summarizers. Fixed or simplistic structures can suppress correct minority contributions, motivating adaptive approaches.

### Graph-Based Optimization

Modeling MAS as computational graphs (agents as nodes, communications as edges) enables topology optimization. GPTSwarm uses RL to optimize interactions and prompts within a graph framework. **OptAgent** advances this via **verbal reinforcement learning**—action spaces, feedback, and policy updates expressed in natural language. A **feedback agent** evaluates interaction quality while an **action agent** modifies the collaboration graph. Connection scores update through a learning rate mechanism. Convergence typically occurs within three epochs, with improvement ceilings dependent on base model reasoning capabilities. Optimal information propagation patterns differ across tasks—e.g., an Explainer agent may need to broadcast first in math but not in creative writing.

### Verbal and Self-Reflective RL

The verbal RL paradigm reflects a broader trend. **Reflexion** established the approach: agents generate natural language reflections on past performance, stored and reused to inform future decisions—leveraging linguistic experience rather than gradient-based optimization. This is valuable when process quality, not just outcomes, matters.

### Cognitive Modeling for Communication

Standard approaches broadcast uniformly without adapting to collaborators' knowledge states. The **Collaborator Knowledge Model (CKM)** addresses this: each agent maintains a per-collaborator cognitive model—a Transformer encoder processing utterances, interaction patterns, and dialogue history into a latent vector of evolving cognitive state, updated each round via a GRU. CKMs are pre-trained on multi-turn dialogue corpora, then fine-tuned end-to-end during RL.

A **cognitive gap analysis module** (cross-attention plus feed-forward layers) computes divergences between an agent's internal state and its collaborator model—identifying factual misunderstandings, reasoning divergences, or goal misalignments—and learns to highlight discrepancies whose resolution most benefits outcomes.

### Communication Policy and Linguistic Realization

A PPO-optimized policy (four-layer Transformer, eight heads, dim 256) determines what to communicate, to whom, with what objective, and in what style. The **composite reward** combines task reward, communication cost penalty, and intrinsic rewards for cognitive gap reductions. Gradients propagate end-to-end through policy, CKM, and gap module over ~500K steps.

A key architectural choice **separates strategic planning from language generation**: the policy outputs abstract action tuples, and a generative LLM serves as a **linguistic realization engine**, translating directives into natural language. This relieves the LLM of autonomous strategic reasoning about collaboration.

### The OSC Framework

**OSC (Orchestrating Cognitive Synergy)** operationalizes CKMs, gap analysis, and PPO-optimized policy as an intermediate collaboration layer—transforming agents from parallel workers into a collaborative cognitive team. Qualitative results show CKM detecting divergent strategies and prompting targeted resolution. Communication metrics show fewer rounds, fewer tokens, lower redundancy, and higher information density versus other frameworks.

### Classical MARL Dynamics in LLM Systems

LLM-based MAS exhibit dynamics analogous to classical multi-agent reinforcement learning: sycophancy, conformity biases, persuasion failures, and collective bias amplification echo the cooperation failures studied under partial observability and conflicting incentives—but manifest through natural language rather than numerical rewards. Frameworks like CAMEL, AutoGen, and ConsensusAgent improve reasoning quality but typically rely on ad hoc reward structures. Effective coordination demands shared, interpretable, and negotiable incentive structures beyond individual agent competence.

**Alignment as a multi-agent problem** reframes the challenge from optimizing individual policies to ensuring cooperative stability. In a manager-worker architecture, the manager elicits stakeholder preferences and coordinates workers through interpretable signals, creating a bilevel optimization: the manager determines objectives, workers optimize them. When workers optimize a proxy utility diverging from true stakeholder utility, the resulting **utility gap** quantifies misalignment. In realistic deployments with closed-source workers, alignment must proceed through delegated control via communication rather than parameter sharing.

### Agent Profiling and Diversity

Distinct profiles (Explainer, Expert, Logical Thinker, etc.) reduce groupthink, with prominent improvement in larger configurations where homogeneous agents converge prematurely. **Forced stochastic decoding** further enhances diversity and outperforms greedy decoding.

### Decision Strategies

**Majority voting** is standard, but a **reconsidering minority** strategy—where unique minority answers trigger group discussion before final voting—is effective when the gap between actual and theoretical upper-bound performance is large.

### Scaling and Cost Trade-offs

Approximately **five to six agents** yield best results. Beyond this, CKM-based systems see ~15% latency increase and ~30% memory growth per step at ten agents due to quadratic CKM scaling; simpler debate systems degrade as deliberation noise overwhelms additional perspectives. Well-optimized frameworks trace a strong Pareto frontier, matching or exceeding GPT-4 variants at lower cost.

### Task-Dependent Effectiveness

Multi-agent debate improves **mathematical reasoning** and **creative writing coherence** but shows limited benefit for **direct knowledge retrieval** or **complex procedural planning** (e.g., sorting). Complex planning may benefit more from specialized external modules—tool use, formal verification, structured search. Multi-agent collaboration is not a universal amplifier but depends on whether the task benefits from reasoning diversity and error correction through discussion.
