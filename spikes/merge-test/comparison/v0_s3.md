## Cosine Similarity and Normalised Dot Product in Embedding Retrieval

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors in a high-dimensional space, defined as the dot product of the vectors divided by the product of their L2 norms: sim(A, B) = A·B / (|A| |B|). The result ranges from −1 (opposite direction) through 0 (orthogonal) to 1 (identical direction). Because the norms cancel out, cosine similarity is scale-invariant — it captures whether two vectors point in the same direction regardless of their magnitudes. This property matters for text and document representation: two pieces of text that discuss the same topics in the same proportions register as similar even if one is substantially longer than the other.

The term "normalised dot product" refers to exactly the same operation. A plain dot product conflates directional similarity with magnitude, but dividing by the norms isolates the angular component. The two names — cosine similarity and normalised dot product — are mathematically identical and fully interchangeable. Different software ecosystems simply surface the operation under different labels: scikit-learn and sentence-transformers expose it as cosine similarity, while FAISS and the OpenAI embeddings API implement it as a dot product applied to pre-normalised vectors.

A key practical optimisation follows from this equivalence. If all embedding vectors are L2-normalised once at index time, every subsequent cosine similarity computation reduces to a plain dot product. This is significant because dot products are what SIMD hardware and approximate nearest-neighbour algorithms — FAISS's IVF indices, HNSW graphs — are optimised to compute at scale, framed as maximum inner product search (MIPS). Major vector databases, including Pinecone, Weaviate, and Qdrant, perform this normalisation automatically when the cosine metric is selected, abstracting the step away from the user while still benefiting from the raw throughput of dot-product kernels.

Cosine similarity, in one guise or the other, is the dominant metric across dense retrieval, semantic search, recommendation systems, and the older TF-IDF retrieval paradigm. Its combination of intuitive geometric meaning, scale invariance, and amenability to hardware-accelerated computation explains its ubiquity.
