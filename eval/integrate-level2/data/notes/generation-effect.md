The generation effect is the finding that self-generated information is better remembered than information simply read or copied. Slamecka and Graf (1978) demonstrated this by having participants either read word pairs (e.g. hot–cold) or generate the second word from the first plus a rule (e.g. hot–c___). Generated words were remembered substantially better on later tests, even when reading time was controlled.

The effect is robust across material types — word pairs, sentences, mathematical operations, conceptual explanations — and holds even when the generation task is trivially easy, such as completing a highly constrained blank. The mere act of producing the target, rather than receiving it, changes the nature of the encoding.

The mechanism appears to involve deeper semantic processing: generating a response requires activating and evaluating candidate answers against meaning constraints, which produces richer encoding than reading the answer passively. This connects the generation effect to levels-of-processing theory — generating involves semantically processing both the cue and the relationship to the target, not just registering the surface form.

Generation also creates a stronger sense of cognitive effort and ownership over the material, which correlates with better memory independent of processing depth. When a learner constructs an explanation in their own words rather than transcribing a received one, they produce something that reflects their own conceptual structure and is therefore more retrievable from their own knowledge network.

**Gap:** the boundary conditions are not fully clear — under what circumstances does generation produce weaker memory (e.g. when the generated response is incorrect and remains uncorrected)?
