## Cosine Similarity and Normalised Dot Product in Embedding Retrieval

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors in a high-dimensional space, independent of their magnitudes. It is defined as the dot product of two vectors divided by the product of their L2 norms:

$$\text{sim}(A, B) = \frac{A \cdot B}{\|A\| \, \|B\|}$$

The result ranges from −1 (opposite direction) through 0 (orthogonality) to 1 (identical direction). This scale invariance is what makes the metric natural for comparing text representations: two documents are considered similar if they discuss the same topics in the same proportions, regardless of length or embedding magnitude.

The terms "cosine similarity" and "normalised dot product" refer to exactly the same mathematical operation. The distinction is purely nominal — different software frameworks expose it under different labels. Scikit-learn and sentence-transformers call it cosine similarity; FAISS, HNSW libraries, and the OpenAI embeddings API implement it as a dot product applied to pre-normalised vectors. Recognising this equivalence is important for avoiding confusion when moving between tools.

A key practical optimisation follows directly from this identity. If all embedding vectors are L2-normalised once at index time, then retrieval at query time can use a plain dot product — which is what FAISS inner-product indices and HNSW graphs are optimised for — while preserving full angular similarity semantics. This conversion from cosine similarity to maximum inner product search (MIPS) enables efficient use of SIMD instructions and hardware-accelerated distance computation. Major vector databases, including Pinecone, Weaviate, and Qdrant, perform this normalisation automatically when the cosine metric is selected, abstracting the step entirely from the user.

The metric originated in classical information retrieval as the standard way to compare TF-IDF vectors and remains the dominant similarity measure across dense retrieval, semantic search, and recommendation systems. Its persistence across paradigm shifts — from sparse bag-of-words models to transformer-based dense embeddings — reflects the robustness of angular comparison as a notion of semantic proximity.
