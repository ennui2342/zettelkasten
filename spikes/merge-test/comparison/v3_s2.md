## Dropout as a Dual-Interpretation Mechanism: Regularization and Bayesian Inference from a Single Procedure

Dropout occupies a rare position in deep learning: a single mathematical operation during training that admits two fully coherent but functionally distinct interpretations, and the choice of interpretation dictates what you do — and what you can extract — at inference time.

Under the regularization interpretation, dropout is a noise injection scheme. Randomly zeroing activations during training prevents co-adaptation among neurons, encouraging distributed representations and acting as an implicit ensemble over exponentially many sub-networks. The key commitment of this framing is that dropout is a *training-time artifact*. Once learning is complete, dropout is disabled, weights are scaled to compensate for the missing activation mass, and the network produces deterministic point predictions. The regularization lens fully explains *why* dropout improves generalization but says nothing about the confidence of any individual prediction.

Under the Bayesian interpretation, the identical dropout procedure is recast as variational inference. Each dropout mask corresponds to a draw from an approximate posterior distribution over network weights, with the Bernoulli dropout rate parameterizing a family of variational distributions. Training with dropout minimizes a variational objective (the evidence lower bound, up to a scaling factor), so the learned weights already encode a posterior approximation. The critical divergence from the regularization view is what follows: dropout should remain active at test time. Multiple stochastic forward passes — Monte Carlo dropout — yield samples from the approximate predictive distribution, and the spread of those samples quantifies epistemic uncertainty.

The bridging insight is that these two interpretations are not competing explanations but *complementary projections* of the same underlying operation onto different theoretical planes. The training dynamics are mathematically identical; what differs is the meta-theoretical commitment about what the dropout mask *represents*. In the regularization frame, the mask is a noise source to be marginalized away after training. In the Bayesian frame, the mask is a latent variable whose posterior variability carries irreducible information about model uncertainty.

This duality has a practical asymmetry: the Bayesian interpretation strictly subsumes the regularization one. Disabling dropout at inference and scaling weights is equivalent to taking the mean of the approximate predictive distribution — a single summary statistic. MC dropout recovers the full distribution, from which the mean can always be extracted but which also provides variance, calibrated confidence intervals, and entropy-based measures of ignorance. The regularization interpretation is therefore a special case — the first moment of what the Bayesian interpretation offers in full.

Recognizing this subsumption relationship clarifies design decisions. If a task only requires point predictions and generalization, the regularization framing is sufficient and computationally cheaper at inference. If a task demands knowing *when the model doesn't know* — active learning, out-of-distribution detection, safety-critical deployment — the Bayesian framing unlocks capabilities that are already latent in the trained model, requiring no architectural change and no retraining, only a different inference protocol.
