The testing effect (also called retrieval practice effect) is the finding that actively recalling information from memory produces substantially stronger long-term retention than passively re-reading or reviewing the same material. The effect is robust across content types, age groups, and delay intervals.

The mechanism is reconstructive: retrieval is not a passive readout of a stable memory but an active reconstruction, which re-encodes the memory in its reconstructed form. This means the retrieval attempt itself is an act of learning, not merely a test of prior learning. Each successful retrieval strengthens the underlying trace and creates additional retrieval pathways.

The practical contrast is stark. In classic experiments, students who took a practice test after reading a passage retained 40–50% more material after one week than students who reread the passage an equivalent number of times, even though rereading felt more productive and the test group's performance in the immediate post-study period was lower.

**Gap:** this note does not cover whether techniques other than direct testing produce equivalent consolidation effects via the same mechanism.
