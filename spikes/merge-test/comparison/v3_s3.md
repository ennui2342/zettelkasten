## Cosine Similarity and Normalised Dot Product in Embedding Retrieval

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors in a high-dimensional space, defined as the dot product of the vectors divided by the product of their L2 norms: sim(A, B) = A·B / (|A| |B|). The result ranges from −1 (diametrically opposite directions) through 0 (orthogonality) to 1 (identical direction). Because the normalization by magnitude removes any dependence on vector length, cosine similarity is scale-invariant: it captures whether two vectors point in the same direction, not whether they have the same size. This property matters for text representations, where two documents discussing the same topics in the same proportions should be judged similar regardless of length differences.

The term "normalised dot product" refers to exactly the same operation. A plain dot product conflates directional similarity with magnitude; dividing by the norms isolates the angular component. The two names — cosine similarity and normalised dot product — are mathematically identical and fully interchangeable. Different software ecosystems simply surface the operation under different labels: scikit-learn and sentence-transformers expose it as cosine similarity, while FAISS and the OpenAI embeddings API implement it as a dot product over pre-normalised vectors.

A key practical optimisation follows from this equivalence. If all embedding vectors are L2-normalised once at index time, every subsequent cosine similarity computation reduces to a plain dot product. Dot products are cheaper to compute, benefit directly from SIMD acceleration, and are the native operation optimised by approximate nearest-neighbour structures such as FAISS IVF indices and HNSW graphs. This transforms the retrieval problem into maximum inner product search (MIPS), for which highly efficient algorithms and hardware paths exist. Major vector databases — including Pinecone, Weaviate, and Qdrant — perform this normalisation automatically when the cosine metric is selected, abstracting the step entirely from the user.

This normalise-then-dot-product pattern is now the standard approach in dense retrieval, semantic search, and recommendation systems, carrying forward a lineage that began with cosine-based TF-IDF retrieval and extends through modern neural embedding pipelines.
