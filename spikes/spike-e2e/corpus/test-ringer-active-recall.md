Active recall and retrieval practice are two names for the same learning strategy: the learner attempts to retrieve target information from memory without consulting the source material, rather than passively reviewing notes or re-reading. The two terms originate from different communities. "Active recall" is the term favoured by self-directed learners, flashcard communities, and productivity writers, having developed empirically among practitioners often without reference to academic work. "Retrieval practice" is the term used in experimental psychology, education research, and the desirable difficulties literature, studied under controlled conditions. Every experimental demonstration of the testing effect simultaneously supports active recall, and every practical implementation of active recall is an instance of retrieval practice. Treating these as separate concepts misrepresents a sociological terminological divergence as a genuine conceptual one; the evidence base is unified and should be represented as such.

The strategy is grounded in the testing effect: retrieving a memory strengthens that memory more than re-studying the same content. Re-reading a textbook or reviewing highlighted notes feels productive but relies on recognition rather than genuine retrieval. Active recall requires the learner to produce an answer from an incomplete or degraded cue, forcing the cognitive system to reconstruct the representation from partial information. This effortful retrieval lays down additional associative pathways and deepens integration of the material with prior knowledge. The advantage over restudying is robust across materials, ages, and retention intervals, and is particularly pronounced at longer delays — a week or more — where the benefit of effortful retrieval over passive review is most apparent.

In experimental research, retrieval practice is typically operationalised through cued recall or free recall tests administered after an initial study phase, with retention assessed at a later delay. In practical settings, the strategy can be implemented through any activity that requires producing information rather than recognising it: flashcards (where the cue is presented and the learner must produce the answer before flipping), practice questions, blank-page recall (closing all materials and writing everything remembered about a topic), the Feynman technique (explaining a concept from memory to identify gaps), or summarising material to another person. The defining feature across all implementations is that the learner generates the target rather than selects from options or reads completed material. The critical element is production from memory, not the specific format of the activity.

Failure during retrieval — where the learner cannot produce the answer — is itself informative, identifying which material requires further study. Feedback after retrieval (confirming correct answers, correcting errors) further enhances the benefit by allowing the learner to update any incorrect reconstructions.

The shared core finding is that generating information from memory produces stronger retention than re-studying the same information, and the shared practical implication is to replace re-reading and passive review with active generation and self-testing. Active recall is widely recommended in self-directed learning communities as the single most effective individual study strategy; retrieval practice is among the most replicated findings in the science of learning. Any knowledge system that encounters both terms should unify them under a single entry acknowledging both names, explaining their different community origins, and presenting their shared mechanism and evidence together.
