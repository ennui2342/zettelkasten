## Mixture of Experts in Language Models

Mixture of Experts (MoE) is a model architecture for scaling language models beyond the computational budgets required by dense transformers. The central design principle involves replacing each feed-forward network (FFN) layer in a transformer with multiple specialised expert FFNs — for example, 64 experts — while employing a learned routing mechanism to direct each input token to only a small subset (top-k) of those experts. Because only a fraction of experts (e.g., 2 out of 64) activate per token, the model's total parameter capacity scales with the number of experts while the floating-point operations per forward pass remain approximately constant. This is the core idea behind conditional computation: decouple parameter count from inference cost.

**Routing Mechanism and Load Balancing**

The router computes a softmax distribution over all available experts for each token and selects the top-k scoring experts. A significant challenge in training MoE models is routing stability: without corrective measures, the router tends to collapse, concentrating nearly all tokens onto a small number of experts (often just 2–3) while leaving the remainder unused. To counteract this, an auxiliary load-balancing loss is introduced during training. This loss penalises imbalanced expert utilisation, typically by adding a term proportional to the variance in how frequently each expert is selected across a training batch. The effect is to encourage the router to distribute tokens more evenly, ensuring that the full capacity of the expert pool is leveraged.

**Expert Specialisation**

A notable emergent property of trained MoE language models is that individual experts develop functional specialisations. Experts tend to become associated with particular token types, syntactic roles, or semantic domains. This specialisation arises organically through training rather than being explicitly programmed. However, the specialisation is partial and coarse-grained — a single expert may handle a diverse range of tokens that share only a broad categorical similarity rather than a narrow topical focus.

**Open Research Directions**

Several fundamental design questions remain active areas of investigation. These include determining the optimal number of experts for a given model scale, selecting the appropriate capacity factor (which governs how many tokens each expert can process per batch), and understanding whether the direction of routing matters in practice — specifically, whether performance differs meaningfully between token-chooses-expert routing and expert-chooses-token routing paradigms.

Related terms: sparse MoE, conditional computation, expert routing, load balancing, capacity factor, top-k routing.
