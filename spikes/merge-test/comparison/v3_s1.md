## Dense Retrieval and Semantic Search via Neural Embedding Spaces

Dense retrieval and semantic search refer to the same underlying technique: encoding queries and documents into dense vector representations within a shared embedding space, then ranking candidates by geometric proximity using metrics such as cosine similarity or dot product. The two terms reflect different disciplinary framings — information retrieval (IR) and natural language processing (NLP) — rather than distinct methods. The architecture, training objectives, and inference procedures are identical across both framings.

The core mechanism is a bi-encoder architecture in which a query encoder and a document encoder (often sharing weights or trained jointly) map natural language text into fixed-dimensional vectors. The embedding space is learned from large text corpora and captures semantic structure: texts with related meaning cluster together regardless of surface-form lexical overlap. A query about "car safety" retrieves documents mentioning "vehicle crash protection" because both occupy nearby regions of the space. This capacity to handle vocabulary mismatch is the principal advantage over sparse retrieval methods such as BM25, which rely on exact or near-exact term matching and fail on out-of-vocabulary queries.

In practice, retrieval over dense vectors is approximate rather than exact. Sub-linear search is achieved through specialised index structures such as HNSW graphs or FAISS indices, which trade a small amount of recall for dramatic gains in query latency at scale. These indices make dense retrieval viable for open-domain question answering, enterprise search, document recommendation, and other applications requiring low-latency access to large corpora.

The convergence of the IR and NLP perspectives became explicit with Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR, Karpukhin et al., 2020), which was recognised in both communities as an embedding-based approach to open-domain question answering. The BEIR benchmark (Thakur et al., 2021) further cemented the equivalence by evaluating systems described under both labels within a single unified protocol.

The only meaningful difference between the two framings is one of emphasis. IR researchers position dense retrieval in contrast to traditional sparse methods, highlighting the shift from term-matching representations to learned dense representations. NLP researchers emphasise the semantic nature of the similarity captured by embeddings, distinguishing it from surface-level or syntactic matching. This terminological divergence has had practical consequences: both communities independently developed closely related techniques — hard negative mining, in-batch negative sampling, training efficiency improvements — sometimes without awareness of parallel work in the other community. Adopting a unified vocabulary across IR and NLP would reduce this redundancy and accelerate progress. Large-scale pretraining remains essential in either framing, as the quality of the embedding space determines retrieval effectiveness.
