# Adversarial Attacks and Safety of LLM-Based Agents

Large language models aligned through reinforcement learning from human feedback or instruction tuning, and optionally augmented with content moderation layers, remain vulnerable to adversarial prompts and jailbreak techniques that circumvent both alignment and moderation. When these models operate as autonomous agents—invoking tools, writing and executing code, navigating browsers, and interacting with complex environments—the attack surface expands well beyond text generation into consequential real-world actions.

### Single-Agent Vulnerabilities

In single-agent settings, adversarial prompting can induce generation and execution of malicious code, harmful browser interactions, and multi-step misuse chains. Critically, agents are also vulnerable through channels other than the user prompt: malicious tool outputs, poisoned memory or knowledge bases, and adversarial inputs embedded in images can trigger harmful behavior even when the user's instructions are entirely benign. Indirect prompt injection—where untrusted content ingested during tool use or retrieval contains adversarial instructions—is a central concern, amplified by computer-use interaction modes that expose agents to arbitrary web content and GUI elements.

### Multi-Agent Attack Surface Expansion

Multi-agent systems introduce qualitatively new risk categories beyond those present in single-agent deployments. Malicious prompts can propagate between agents through shared communication channels. Attacks can exploit the specialization and collaboration dynamics inherent in multi-agent coordination—compromised or rogue agents can undermine system integrity from within.

A particularly insidious class of vulnerability arises from **context fragmentation**. When an orchestrator decomposes a harmful task into atomic instructions, individual sub-agents may lack sufficient context to recognize the collective harmful intent of the decomposed steps. This distributed responsibility means that a task a single agent would refuse can be completed when spread across multiple agents, none of which sees the full picture. The fragmentation effect is amplified by low sub-agent autonomy: when orchestrators issue fine-grained atomic commands rather than high-level subtasks, each worker operates with minimal contextual awareness, reducing its ability to assess whether its contribution participates in a harmful workflow.

### Vulnerability Patterns in Centralized Architectures

Several specific failure modes have been identified in centralized multi-agent systems where an orchestrator coordinates specialized workers:

- **Inadequate fallback mechanisms.** When a planner refuses to generate a plan but the system lacks explicit halt instructions, subsequent sub-agents may still be invoked and exposed to the harmful task unnecessarily. The absence of a propagating stop signal means refusal at one stage does not guarantee system-level safety.

- **Blind plan execution.** Stratified planning architectures—where plan construction occurs as a distinct phase before execution—can lead orchestrators to mechanically execute pre-generated plans without reassessing safety during the execution phase. Once a plan exists, the execution engine treats it as authoritative regardless of whether individual steps, viewed in sequence, reveal harmful intent that was obscured during decomposition.

- **Sub-agent refusal override.** Orchestrators may occasionally ignore sub-agent refusals and proceed through alternative delegation paths, effectively breaking the safety hierarchy. If one worker refuses a subtask, the orchestrator routes it to another worker or reformulates the request, circumventing the refusal signal that should have triggered system-level reconsideration.

- **Unmitigated execution.** In a significant proportion of cases, no agent in the pipeline—neither the planner nor any sub-agent—recognizes or refuses the harmful request. This indicates that alignment techniques developed for single-model interactions do not reliably transfer to multi-agent contexts, where the distribution of responsibility dilutes each agent's sense of accountability.

### Model Scale and Inverted Vulnerability Patterns

Smaller language models exhibit vulnerability patterns that are inverted relative to frontier models. Planning-level defenses systematically fail in smaller models—they readily generate harmful plans—while sub-agent-level defenses remain more effective for explicitly harmful tasks, as smaller models are more likely to refuse direct execution of overtly dangerous instructions. However, smaller models show heightened vulnerability to code-based attacks, where both planning and execution phases proceed without any safety intervention. This asymmetry suggests that the cognitive demands of plan-level safety assessment (recognizing that a sequence of individually innocuous steps constitutes a harmful workflow) exceed smaller models' capabilities, while pattern-matching against explicitly harmful content remains within reach.

### Defensive Approaches

Defenses span multiple layers of the agent pipeline:

- **Guard agents** operating from explicit safety specifications can intercept harmful requests before they reach execution. These function as dedicated safety-checking components rather than relying on the task-performing agents' own alignment.

- **Adaptive safety checks** adjust scrutiny based on task characteristics, applying more intensive evaluation to requests matching risk profiles rather than applying uniform overhead to all interactions.

- **Probabilistic logical reasoning shields** combine formal reasoning with probabilistic assessment to evaluate whether proposed actions violate safety constraints, offering more principled guarantees than purely heuristic approaches.

- **Control and data flow extraction** with fine-grained security policy enforcement analyzes the structural properties of agent workflows—what data flows where, which tools are invoked in what sequence—and enforces policies at the flow level rather than the individual-message level.

- **Response filtering** applies output-stage evaluation to catch harmful content that bypassed input-stage defenses, providing defense in depth.

- **Agent message challenging mechanisms** require agents to justify or defend their outputs to skeptical evaluators before those outputs propagate through the system, creating adversarial pressure against harmful content within the agent pipeline itself.

- **Memory seeding** populates agent memory with examples of safe handling of malicious inputs, leveraging in-context learning to bias agents toward safe responses when encountering similar patterns. This approach connects to the broader finding that dense legitimate memory entries provide natural resilience against poisoning attacks by diluting adversarial content in retrieval.

The fundamental challenge is that multi-agent systems create emergent safety properties (and failures) that cannot be predicted from the safety characteristics of individual agents. A system composed entirely of individually aligned agents can still produce harmful outcomes through context fragmentation, inadequate inter-agent safety signaling, and the distributed dilution of responsibility. Effective defense requires treating safety as a system-level property requiring system-level mechanisms—not merely a property of individual model alignment that automatically composes.
