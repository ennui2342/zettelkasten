# Benchmark Suites and Empirical Performance Landscape for Agentic AI

A growing ecosystem of benchmarks targets specific capability slices of agentic AI, from single-step reasoning to multi-agent negotiation and enterprise workflow automation. Mapping these benchmarks against empirical model performance reveals where current systems excel, where they plateau, and where architectural choices dominate model scale.

### Benchmark Catalog by Capability

**Task performance and accuracy.** General-purpose assistants: **GAIA** (165 tasks across three tiers, evaluating multi-step decomposition and verification on tasks easy for humans but hard for agents), **BrowseComp+** (830 deep web research tasks). Desktop and OS control: **OSWorld** and **OSWorld Verified** benchmark full desktop control with reduced evaluation noise. Code generation and software engineering: **HumanEval**, **MBPP**, **DS-1000** (pass@1); **SWE-bench** and **SWE-bench Verified** assess software engineering capabilities, with **SWE-bench Pro** pushing toward repository-scale longer-horizon tasks. Mathematical reasoning: **GSM8K** (solve rate), **MATH** (harder, high-school level across six fields)—well-suited for testing collaborative reasoning gains; **FrontierMath** targets hard-ceiling mathematical reasoning that current systems cannot reliably solve. Adversarial math variants: **AdversarialGSM** (modified number magnitudes, three difficulty levels) and **GSM-PLUS** (multiple perturbation categories including "critical thinking"). Science: **GPQA** (graduate-level), **ARC** (knowledge retrieval more than step-by-step reasoning). QA: **HotpotQA**, **DROP** (F1). Multi-turn tool interaction: **τ-bench** evaluates agents under policy constraints across extended interactions. Domain-specific: **DDXPlus** (medical), **Shopping-MMLU** (e-commerce—screens tasks using a strong model, retaining only those below an accuracy threshold). Planning: **TravelPlanner** (task success rate). Additional: numerical sorting at varying lengths (error scope metric), creative writing (coherence 1–10 via LLM rubrics).

**Goal-directed file-based evaluation.** Benchmarks like **GDPVal** evaluate agents on multi-step work episodes where outputs are verifiable artifacts—Markdown files, spreadsheets (XLSX/CSV), documents (PDF/DOCX), or images (PNG/JPG)—rather than language-only responses. This file-based output format enables deterministic verification by downstream evaluators, providing more objective assessment than purely subjective text evaluation. Such benchmarks are particularly suited to domains like data analysis, document authoring, and visual reporting.

**Multi-agent evaluation.** **AgentBench** evaluates agents across diverse environments. **MultiAgentBench (MARBLE)** measures emergent multi-agent behaviors such as negotiation efficiency and consensus formation, moving beyond individual task success to collective dynamics.

**Instruction-following and dialogue.** **AlpacaEval 2.0** (805 instructions, length-controlled win rate vs. GPT-4 Preview). **MT-Bench** (multi-turn dialogue, first/second turn scores).

**Joint architectural evaluation.** **AgentArch** tests 18 configurations across six LLMs (GPT-4.1, GPT-4o, GPT-4.1-mini, Claude Sonnet 4, o3-mini, LLaMA 3.3 70B) on two enterprise workflows—simpler time-off (8 tools, 3 agents) and complex customer routing (31 tools, 9 agents)—with 60 deliberately complex test samples each.

**Enterprise benchmarks.** **WorkBench**, **CRMArena**, **WorkArena**, **MLE-Bench**, **StreamBench** document the academic-to-real-world gap but tend to evaluate components in isolation rather than end-to-end workflows.

### Empirical Performance and Enterprise Deployment Realities

Stronger base models narrow the gap between simple and sophisticated methods—GPT-4o with simple debate achieves near-perfect GSM8K performance, leaving little room for framework optimization. Mixed-model performance is heavily influenced by constituent backbone capabilities.

Peak enterprise performance: 70.8% on simpler workflows (GPT-4.1), 35.3% on complex ones (Claude Sonnet 4). Highest pass^k: 6.34%—consistent repeatable success remains far from enterprise-ready. Larger models show low coefficients of variation (GPT-4.1: 27.0%, Claude Sonnet 4: 32.1%); some exhibit extreme sensitivity (o3-mini CV: 143.7%). Smaller models can match larger ones on simpler tasks with appropriate architectures (GPT-4.1-mini: 67.1% vs. Claude Sonnet 4: 68.5% on time-off), reinforcing the cost-performance tradeoff: knowledge distillation and architecture-task matching can deliver adequate accuracy at a fraction of the computational budget.

Enterprise teams should test multiple configurations against specific use cases, evaluating task performance, communication quality, latency under realistic asynchronous conditions, and failure severity—not just mean accuracy.
