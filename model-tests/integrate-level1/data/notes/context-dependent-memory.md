Context-dependent memory is the finding that memory is better when the conditions at retrieval match the conditions at encoding. Godden and Baddeley's classic study had divers learn word lists either on land or underwater, then tested them in the same or different environment: recall was 40% better when tested in the matching environment. The physical setting becomes part of the encoded representation and functions as a retrieval cue at test.

The same principle applies to internal states. Mood-congruent retrieval — the tendency to recall mood-consistent material more readily — reflects encoding of the emotional context alongside the content. Material learned while anxious is recalled better in anxious states; material learned in a positive mood is more accessible in positive states.

The mechanism is encoding specificity (Tulving's encoding specificity principle): whatever is present in the cognitive environment at encoding tends to become part of the memory trace and can later serve as a retrieval cue. This includes physical surroundings, internal states, associated thoughts, and concurrent sensory information.

The practical implication runs in two directions. For deliberate learning, varied practice conditions make memories less dependent on any single context — the material becomes accessible across a wider range of retrieval environments. For memory failure, apparent forgetting may sometimes be context-mismatch rather than storage loss: testing in a different context than encoding degrades access even when the information is intact.
